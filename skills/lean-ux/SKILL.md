# Lean UX

## Scope

Lean UX applies lean thinking to user experience design, shifting from deliverable-heavy design processes (wireframes, specs, handoffs) to outcome-focused, hypothesis-driven, cross-functional collaboration. It is the design-side complement to lean startup.

## Core Concepts

### Outcomes Over Outputs

Traditional UX measures success by deliverables produced (wireframes delivered, specs written). Lean UX measures success by outcomes achieved (user behavior changed, business metric improved). The question shifts from "did we ship the feature?" to "did the feature produce the intended result?"

### Hypothesis-Driven Design

Every design decision is framed as a testable hypothesis:

**Format:** "We believe that [this capability/change] for [these users] will achieve [this outcome]. We'll know we're right when we see [this measurable signal]."

This format forces clarity: What exactly are we building? For whom? What outcome do we expect? How will we know? Without this structure, design decisions default to opinion, politics, or the HiPPO (highest-paid person's opinion).

### Experiment Maps

Visual planning tools that organize hypotheses by risk and learning priority. The experiment map shows which assumptions are riskiest (highest uncertainty x highest impact), which experiments will test them, and what order to run them. Start with the riskiest assumption -- if it's wrong, everything built on it is waste.

### Cross-Functional Collaboration

Lean UX breaks down the designer-developer-PM handoff model. Instead, small cross-functional teams work together through the entire cycle: define the hypothesis together, design the experiment together, build it together, measure results together. Design is a team activity, not a deliverable from a design department.

### Minimum Viable Design

The design equivalent of MVP. The minimum design investment needed to test a hypothesis. This might be a paper sketch, a clickable prototype, a Wizard of Oz simulation, or a single-screen implementation. Polish comes after validation, not before.

## The Lean UX Cycle

1. **Declare assumptions** -- What do we believe about our users, their problems, and our solution?
2. **Create hypotheses** -- Frame the riskiest assumptions as testable hypotheses.
3. **Design experiments** -- What's the cheapest, fastest way to test each hypothesis? (MVP, prototype, A/B test, user interview)
4. **Run experiments** -- Build the minimum, expose it to real users, measure.
5. **Learn and decide** -- Did the data confirm or refute the hypothesis? Pivot, persevere, or iterate.

## Relationship to Other Lean Traditions

- **Lean Startup:** Lean UX is lean startup applied specifically to the design dimension. BML loop is the shared operating model.
- **TPS:** Lean UX treats unnecessary design deliverables as overproduction (muda), handoff-driven workflows as transport waste, and spec-heavy processes as inventory.
- **Lean Software Development:** Lean UX's emphasis on deferring decisions (last responsible moment) and set-based design (exploring multiple solutions before converging) connects directly to Poppendieck's principles.

## When This Tradition Applies

- **Problem type:** Type 4 (what to design is uncertain) and Type 3 (pursuing a specific UX target condition).
- **Context signals:** Product design, service design, UX improvement, interface redesign, any situation where user behavior and experience are the primary concerns.

## Common Mistakes

- **Lean UX as "skip research."** Lean UX is not an excuse to skip user research -- it's a framework for doing research faster and more iteratively. No research is not lean; it's negligent.
- **Hypotheses as opinions.** "We believe users will love the new dashboard" is not testable. Hypotheses require specific, measurable outcomes.
- **Prototyping without testing.** Building prototypes but not putting them in front of users defeats the purpose. The prototype is a learning vehicle, not a deliverable.
