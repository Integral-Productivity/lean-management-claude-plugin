# Job Instruction (TWI)

## What It Is

Job Instruction (JI) is the TWI program for teaching people how to do a job correctly, safely, and efficiently. It provides a structured four-step method for skill transfer that is dramatically more effective than "watch me and figure it out" or "read the manual."

JI is the link between standardized work (the documented method) and actual worker capability. Without effective instruction, standardized work is a document that exists but isn't followed. JI ensures people can actually perform the standard -- and understand why each step matters.

## When to Use

- **Problem type alignment:** Types 1, 2. Type 1: when a standard isn't being followed because training was inadequate. Type 2: when quality or consistency gaps trace back to skill deficits or inconsistent practices.
- **Phase alignment:** Phase 5 (Implementation -- training people on new countermeasures) and Phase 6 (Standardization -- ensuring new standards are taught effectively).
- **ADP alignment:** Prevention. Well-trained workers following well-designed methods prevent errors at source.

## Preparation: The Job Breakdown Sheet

Before teaching, the instructor creates a Job Breakdown Sheet with three columns:

1. **Important Steps** -- The major steps of the job in sequence. Each step advances the work.
2. **Key Points** -- For each step, the critical details that affect quality, safety, or technique. "What makes or breaks this step."
3. **Reasons** -- Why each key point matters. The reason connects the detail to its consequence -- without reasons, key points are rules to memorize; with reasons, they're principles to understand.

The Job Breakdown Sheet comes directly from `standardized-work` but is formatted for teaching, not for reference. It captures the instructor's expert knowledge in teachable form.

## The JI Four-Step Method

### Step 1: Prepare the Learner

- Put the learner at ease. Anxiety blocks learning.
- State the job and find out what the learner already knows.
- Get the learner interested -- explain why this job matters and how it connects to the larger process.
- Position the learner where they can see the job being done.

### Step 2: Present the Operation

Demonstrate the job to the learner -- not once, but three times, each with a different focus:

- **First pass:** Demonstrate the complete job at normal speed while describing the **Important Steps** only. This gives the big picture.
- **Second pass:** Demonstrate again, this time calling out each **Key Point** for each step. Slow down to emphasize these.
- **Third pass:** Demonstrate again, this time explaining the **Reason** for each key point. This is where understanding develops.

The three-pass method is the core innovation of JI. It layers information progressively rather than overwhelming the learner with everything at once.

### Step 3: Try Out Performance

- Have the learner do the job while the instructor observes.
- **First attempt:** Learner performs silently. Instructor corrects errors.
- **Second attempt:** Learner performs while stating the Important Steps.
- **Third attempt:** Learner performs while stating Key Points.
- **Fourth attempt:** Learner performs while explaining Reasons.

This progression verifies not just that the learner can perform the job, but that they understand why each element matters. A learner who can explain the reasons will make better decisions when unusual situations arise.

### Step 4: Follow Up

- Designate a person the learner can go to for help.
- Check on the learner frequently at first, then reduce frequency as competence builds.
- Encourage questions. Make it safe to ask for help.
- Gradually taper off support as the learner demonstrates consistent, independent capability.

## Adaptation for Knowledge Work

JI's four-step method translates directly to onboarding, pair programming, mentoring, and any skill transfer:

- **Job Breakdown Sheet -> Procedure with rationale:** Document the process steps, the critical decisions/details at each step, and why they matter. Not just "click here" but "enter the value here because this field drives the downstream calculation."
- **Three-pass demonstration -> Screen share / pair work:** First pass shows the complete workflow. Second pass highlights the critical decision points. Third pass explains the reasoning behind each decision.
- **Tryout -> Supervised practice:** The learner performs the task while narrating their thinking. The instructor listens for understanding gaps, not just procedural errors.
- **Follow-up -> Scheduled check-ins:** Especially critical in remote settings where the learner can't easily ask for help.

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping preparation.** Teaching without a Job Breakdown Sheet means relying on memory, which leads to inconsistent instruction and omitted key points.
- **One-pass demonstration.** Showing the job once and saying "got it?" overwhelms the learner. The three-pass method exists because learning is layered.
- **Skipping the tryout.** Demonstrating without having the learner practice is lecture, not instruction. Performance is where learning happens.
- **No follow-up.** Assuming the learner is competent after one training session. Skills consolidate through practice with feedback.
- **Teaching without explaining reasons.** Workers who know WHAT to do but not WHY will make poor decisions when the situation deviates from standard.

## Relationship to Other Tools

- **standardized-work:** JI uses the standardized work as its content source. Without standardized work, there's nothing consistent to teach.
- **job-methods:** JI teaches the current method; JM improves it. When JM produces a new method, JI retrains workers on it.
- **job-relations:** Effective instruction requires a positive relationship. JR's "put the learner at ease" principle is embedded in JI Step 1.
- **leader-standard-work:** Training and competency verification are LSW activities.
- **visual-management:** Job Breakdown Sheets posted at point of use serve as both training aids and reference standards.
