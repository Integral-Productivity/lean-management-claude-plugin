# Job Relations (TWI)

## What It Is

Job Relations (JR) is the TWI program for handling people problems and building positive working relationships. It provides supervisors and leaders with a structured four-step method for addressing interpersonal issues, conflicts, and behavioral concerns before they escalate -- while maintaining respect for the individual and the relationship.

JR is foundational to lean's "respect for people" pillar. The Toyota Production System depends on workers who feel safe surfacing problems (andon), contributing improvement ideas (kaizen), and challenging the status quo (`job-methods`). None of this happens without trust, and trust requires skilled relationship management.

## When to Use

- **Problem type alignment:** Relevant across all four types. People problems surface in every kind of improvement work -- resistance to change, interpersonal conflict, unclear expectations, disengagement, anxiety about new methods.
- **Phase alignment:** JR is not a phase-specific tool -- it is an ongoing leadership practice. However, it becomes especially critical during Phase 5 (Implementation) when changes affect people's work, and during coaching interactions at any phase.
- **ADP alignment:** Prevention-oriented when practiced proactively (building relationships before problems arise). Detection-oriented when used to surface emerging problems early.

Use Job Relations when:
- A team member's behavior or performance has changed
- Conflict exists between individuals or between a person and a new process/standard
- Resistance to a lean improvement is rooted in relationship or trust issues rather than technical disagreement
- A leader needs to have a difficult conversation about performance, expectations, or change
- Morale or engagement is declining without obvious technical cause

## The JR Four-Step Method

### Step 1: Get the Facts

Gather all relevant facts before taking action. Facts include:
- What happened specifically? (Observable behavior, not interpretations)
- What is the person's work history and track record?
- What rules, standards, or expectations apply?
- What are the circumstances -- workload, personal situation, recent changes?
- What have others observed?

**Critical:** Talk to the individual involved. JR insists on getting the person's perspective directly, not relying on secondhand accounts. Ask open-ended questions. Listen fully before forming a judgment.

Review the record of the relationship -- past interactions, prior conversations, any pattern. Be sure you have the whole story.

### Step 2: Weigh and Decide

Consider the full picture before choosing a course of action:
- What are the possible actions? (Generate multiple options, not just the first idea.)
- What is the effect of each action on the individual? On the team? On production/output?
- Does the action align with the organization's values and policies?
- Does it address the actual problem, or just the symptom?
- Will it maintain or strengthen the relationship, or damage it?

**Key principle:** Consider the effect on the individual first, then the group. Lean's respect-for-people pillar means the person is not subordinate to the process.

### Step 3: Take Action

Act on your decision. JR emphasizes:
- Don't pass the buck -- handle it yourself if it's yours to handle. Only escalate if the situation genuinely requires a higher level of authority.
- Timing matters -- act promptly, but not impulsively. Step 1 and Step 2 prevent impulsive action.
- Be direct and honest. Vague feedback or indirect communication creates confusion and erodes trust.
- The action should match the severity of the situation. Not every problem requires a formal conversation; not every serious issue can be handled casually.

### Step 4: Check Results

Follow up to verify the action worked:
- Did the behavior or situation change?
- How is the individual responding? Is the relationship intact?
- Did the action create any unintended consequences?
- Is the problem resolved, or has it shifted form?

If the problem persists or the relationship has been damaged, return to Step 1 with what you've learned.

## Foundations for Good Relations

JR also teaches four proactive practices that prevent many people problems from arising:

1. **Let each worker know how they are doing.** People cannot improve without feedback. Regular, specific, balanced feedback -- not annual reviews -- is the standard.
2. **Give credit when due.** Recognition is not flattery -- it is acknowledgment of genuine contribution. Specific recognition ("your suggestion to rearrange the approval sequence saved two hours per week") is more effective than general praise.
3. **Tell people in advance about changes that will affect them.** Surprise changes generate resistance. Even when the change is beneficial, being told about it after the fact signals disrespect. This is especially important during lean implementation -- every improvement changes someone's work.
4. **Make the best use of each person's ability.** People who are underutilized disengage. People who are overwhelmed burn out. Match work to capability and interest, and develop people toward greater capability over time.

## Adaptation for Knowledge Work

JR translates directly to knowledge work, remote teams, and coaching contexts:

- "Getting the facts" in remote settings means scheduled one-on-one conversations, not drive-by Slack messages. Written communication loses tone and invites misinterpretation -- voice or video for sensitive conversations.
- "Changes that will affect them" includes tool changes, process changes, reporting structure changes, and even changes to meeting cadence. In lean transformations, these are frequent.
- In coaching contexts, JR provides the relationship foundation that makes developmental feedback possible. A coaching relationship without JR's proactive practices will struggle with the vulnerability required for growth.

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping Step 1 (facts).** Acting on assumptions, rumors, or a single perspective. JR insists on hearing from the person directly before deciding.
- **Treating symptoms as problems.** Someone showing up late may be dealing with a schedule conflict, a childcare issue, a commute change, or disengagement. The behavior is the symptom; the cause determines the response.
- **Avoiding difficult conversations.** JR does not mean "be nice." It means "be respectful AND honest." Avoiding a necessary conversation is not respect -- it's conflict avoidance that allows problems to compound.
- **Failing to follow up (Step 4).** Taking action and assuming it worked. Without checking results, problems go underground rather than getting resolved.
- **Applying JR only when problems exist.** The four foundations are proactive practices. Leaders who only engage with people when there's a problem have already failed at JR.

## Relationship to Other Tools

- **leader-standard-work:** JR's four foundations should be embedded in leader standard work -- regular one-on-ones, gemba conversations, and recognition are not extras but core leadership practices.
- **gemba:** Gemba is where JR happens naturally. Walking the floor is as much about relationship-building as process observation.
- **kaizen-events:** JR creates the trust environment that makes kaizen suggestions possible. Without trust, people protect the status quo.
- **job-instruction:** JR's "let each worker know how they are doing" aligns with the feedback component of JI's training follow-up.
- **a3:** When a people-related problem is complex enough to warrant structured analysis, JR's four steps can be documented in A3 format.
