# Daily Accountability Practices

## What It Is

Daily accountability is the set of structured, recurring practices that connect daily work performance to targets, surface problems quickly, and ensure follow-through on countermeasures. The core mechanism is the tiered daily huddle -- short, focused team meetings at each management level, cascading information up and actions down.

Daily accountability is not micromanagement. It is the management system that makes problems visible at the pace of work rather than at the pace of monthly reviews. Without it, problems accumulate silently until they become crises.

## When to Use

- **Problem type alignment:** Types 1, 2. Daily accountability surfaces deviations from standard (Type 1 triggers) and persistent gaps (Type 2 patterns) through regular review.
- **Phase alignment:** Ongoing practice. Supports Phase 2 (Current Condition -- daily data collection) and Phase 6 (Verification -- sustaining improvements through regular review).
- **ADP alignment:** Detection. Daily review against targets is a systematic detection mechanism.

## Tiered Huddle Structure

### Tier 1: Team Huddle (5-15 minutes, daily)

**Participants:** Frontline team + team lead.
**Timing:** Start of shift or start of day.
**At the board:** Team gathers at their visual management board (not in a conference room, not on a video call if avoidable).

**Agenda (3 elements):**
1. **Review yesterday's performance.** Key metrics vs. targets. What was green (on target)? What was red (missed)? Use the performance board -- don't rely on memory or reports.
2. **Address reds.** For each miss: What happened? What's the immediate countermeasure? Who owns it? When will it be done? If the team can resolve it, assign it. If not, escalate to Tier 2.
3. **Today's plan.** Any special conditions, changes, or priorities for today? Who needs support?

**Not in scope:** Long problem-solving discussions, project updates, information sharing that isn't action-oriented. These happen in other forums.

### Tier 2: Department/Area Huddle (10-20 minutes, daily)

**Participants:** Tier 1 leaders + area manager.
**Timing:** 30-60 minutes after Tier 1 huddles (allows escalations to arrive).

**Agenda:**
1. **Review escalations from Tier 1.** What couldn't be resolved at the team level? What cross-functional issues surfaced?
2. **Review area-level metrics.** Multi-team trends, multi-day patterns.
3. **Assign cross-functional countermeasures.** Who owns what? When will it be resolved?
4. **Escalate to Tier 3** anything requiring senior leadership decision, resources, or authority.

### Tier 3: Senior Leadership Review (15-30 minutes, weekly or bi-weekly)

**Participants:** Area managers + senior leader.

**Agenda:**
1. **Review persistent escalations.** What problems have been raised that the organization hasn't resolved?
2. **Review improvement initiative progress.** Active A3s, kaizen events, strategic projects.
3. **Strategy alignment.** Are daily problems signaling strategic issues?

## The Escalation Chain

The tiered structure creates a disciplined escalation path:
- Problems raised at Tier 1 that can't be resolved -> escalate to Tier 2
- Problems at Tier 2 that require authority or resources -> escalate to Tier 3
- **Critical rule:** Escalated problems must get a response. If Tier 2 doesn't respond to a Tier 1 escalation within the defined timeframe (typically 24-48 hours), the system loses credibility. Track escalation response times as a system health metric.

## Design Principles

- **At the board, not at a desk.** Huddles happen at the visual management board. Standing, not sitting. The board is the focal point, not a slide deck.
- **Time-boxed.** Huddles that run long lose participants and energy. Use a timer. If a topic needs deeper discussion, take it offline.
- **Problem-focused, not reporting-focused.** The question is "what's abnormal and what are we doing about it?" not "let's go around and give updates."
- **Leader as facilitator, not lecturer.** The team lead facilitates; team members report on their own metrics and issues.
- **Consistent cadence, no exceptions.** Daily means daily. Skipping huddles when things are "fine" means you only meet when things are bad -- which turns huddles into crisis meetings.

## Common Mistakes

- **Huddles without boards.** Verbal-only huddles are unanchored and forgettable. The board creates shared visibility and persistent memory.
- **Status reporting instead of problem-solving.** "Everything's on track" for 15 minutes is not a huddle -- it's a ritual. Focus on the reds.
- **No follow-through on countermeasures.** Assigning actions without tracking completion. Yesterday's actions should be reviewed at today's huddle.
- **Blaming individuals for missed targets.** Daily accountability surfaces system problems. If the response is blame, people will stop surfacing problems.
- **Tier 1 without Tier 2/3 support.** If escalations disappear into a void, frontline teams learn that escalating is pointless.
